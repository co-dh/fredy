# ¬ß1.28 ‚Äî proto-morphisms and the idempotent-splitting category

Notes on Freyd & Scedrov, *Categories and Allegories* ¬ß1.28. Composition in **diagram order**:
`Ax` means first `A` then `x`.

## What a proto-morphism is (general, ¬ß1.2)

A category can be presented by a **two-sorted theory**: one sort for **objects**, one sort for
**proto-morphisms**. A proto-morphism is a *raw arrow-datum* ‚Äî a candidate arrow that does not yet
have a fixed source/target.

What turns a proto-morphism into an actual morphism is the **source-target predicate** (a.k.a. arrow
predicate) `A ‚Äîx‚Üí B`, read "`x` may be construed as going from `A` to `B`". The *same* proto-morphism
`x` may satisfy that predicate for several object pairs, or for none.

The category one actually obtains (¬ß1.22) has as its **morphisms** the *instances* of the predicate ‚Äî
triples `(A, x, B)`, not the bare `x`.

> proto-morphism = the underlying arrow data; morphism = that data together with a chosen
> source and target.

Convention: upper-case variables for objects, lower-case for proto-morphisms.

## In ¬ß1.28 (the idempotent-splitting / Karoubi category)

¬ß1.28 builds a category out of a class `E` of idempotents in a category `A`, presented via the
¬ß1.2‚Äì1.22 machinery:

- **objects** = the idempotents in `E`,
- **proto-morphisms** = the morphisms of the original category `A` (reused as raw arrows),
- **source-target predicate**: for idempotents `A, B` (upper-case = idempotents) and a proto-morphism
  `x`,

  ```
  A ‚Äîx‚Üí B   iff   Ax = x = xB
  ```

  In diagram order: `e_A ; x = x` and `x ; e_B = x` ‚Äî `x` is absorbed by `e_A` on the left and by
  `e_B` on the right.

So the proto-morphisms here are just **the arrows of `A`**, and the predicate `Ax = x = xB` selects
which of them count as morphisms between two idempotent-objects.

### Consequences noted in the text

- The **identity** on an object `A` (an idempotent) is `A` *itself*: `A¬∑A = A = A¬∑A` makes `A ‚ÄîA‚Üí A`
  hold, and `A` acts as the identity. This is why the forgetful map back to `A` is **not** generally a
  functor ‚Äî when the idempotents are not all identities (`E ‚äÑ |A|`) it fails to preserve identities.
- Each idempotent `e ‚àà E` **splits** canonically in this category (¬ß1.281): `e = xy`, `yx = 1`. That
  is the whole point ‚Äî this is the Karoubi / idempotent-completion construction. (¬ß1.282: any two
  splittings of the same idempotent are canonically isomorphic.)

## Which side is the fixed point?

It is **`x`** that is the fixed point; `A` and `B` are the idempotents acting *on* it. Read
`Ax = x = xB` as two fixed-point conditions on `x`:

- `Ax = x` ‚Äî `x` is fixed by `A` acting **on the left** (pre-composition). `A` behaves like an
  identity for `x` on the **source** side.
- `xB = x` ‚Äî `x` is fixed by `B` acting **on the right** (post-composition). `B` behaves like an
  identity for `x` on the **target** side.

So `x` is a fixed point of *both* operators "left-multiply by `A`" and "right-multiply by `B`". The
idempotents `A`, `B` are the would-be identities; `x` is the arrow they fix from either side ‚Äî which
is exactly what makes `A` the left identity (source) and `B` the right identity (target) of `x` in the
new category. (Saying "`A` is `x`'s fixed point" reverses the roles: `A` is the operator, `x` is what
is held fixed.)

## Why this is element-free, and why that matters

The presentation is **arrows-only**: `A` and `x` live in the *same* sort (proto-morphisms), so both
`Ax` and `xB` are just composition ‚Äî you can multiply on the **left** and on the **right**. If `x`
were an *element* of a set and `A` a function, you would only have one-sided application `x(A)`, and
there would be no way to say "fix `x` from the right." The two-sided absorption is only expressible
because everything is an arrow.

This is exactly why categories generalize monoids, and why objects can be **identified with their
identity arrows**: an identity is characterized purely equationally by `1x = x = x1`, with no
reference to what `x` "does" to anything. Source and target are recovered as *the* left/right
absorbing idempotents ‚Äî no elements, no application.

¬ß1.28 is the payoff of taking that seriously: since objects are just two-sided identities, any
idempotent that *acts* like a two-sided identity (`Ax = x = xB`) is allowed to *be* an object. Drop
the requirement that identities be "the real" ones and let every idempotent in `E` serve as an object.
The element-free formulation is what makes that move even expressible.

## Does every idempotent split? (and is splitting an AC property?)

Idempotent splitting is **not** automatic, and it has **nothing to do with AC regularity**.

An idempotent `e : A ‚Üí A` *splits* if there are `x : A ‚Üí B`, `y : B ‚Üí A` with `xy = e` and `yx = 1_B`.
A category in which all idempotents split is called **idempotent-complete** (a.k.a. Cauchy / Karoubi
complete) ‚Äî a special property, not something a general category has.

The `ùíÆplit(E)` construction of ¬ß1.28 is exactly the device that *forces* splitting: adjoin the
idempotents as objects, and each `e ‚àà E` then splits canonically (¬ß1.281). If `E` = all idempotents,
all idempotents split in the completed category. (¬ß1.573 does precisely this for the primitive-recursive
functions: that category lacks equalizers, so idempotents are split by hand to build `P`.)

### But in a Cartesian category they split for free ‚Äî no AC needed

**Any category with equalizers splits all of its idempotents**, and a Cartesian category has
equalizers. The construction is the one used in `ac_factorization_via_idempotent` (¬ß1.571):

Given idempotent `e : A ‚Üí A`, let `m : C ‚Üí A` be the equalizer of `id_A` and `e`. Since
`e;e = e = e;id`, the map `e` factors through that equalizer: `p;m = e` with `p : A ‚Üí C`. Then

- `m;p = id_C`  (because `m;p;m = m;e = m = id;m` and `m` is monic), and
- `p;m = e`.

So `e` splits as `(p, m)`, with `p` left-invertible. **This uses only the equalizer, i.e.
Cartesianness ‚Äî no AC, no "same level".**

### Where AC actually comes in

Splitting alone gives the *left-invertible* leg `p`. Being **AC regular** (¬ß1.57) is stronger: *every*
morphism must factor as left-invertible `;` **monic**. The ¬ß1.571 hypothesis (an idempotent `e` with
`ex = x` *and the same level as `x`*) is what upgrades "idempotents split" to "AC regular": the
splitting handles `p`, and the same-level condition is the extra ingredient forcing the second leg
`n = m;x` to be **monic** (see `Fredy/S1_57.md`).

| Property                | What it needs                                                  |
|-------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------|
| Every idempotent splits | equalizers (‚áê Cartesian), or do ¬ß1.28's `ùíÆplit(E)` by hand    |
| AC regular              | the above **plus** ¬ß1.571's same-level idempotents (the AC content) |
