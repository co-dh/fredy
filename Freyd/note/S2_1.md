## Integrity note: SemiSimple recip direction (fixed)

The original `SemiSimple` encoded `R = F ≫ G°` (F : a→c, G : b→c, common *target*).
That is the WRONG reciprocal: Freyd §2.16(10) defines a semi-simple morphism as `R = F°G`
with F, G simple, and §2.143 fixes the typing — a tabulation `(F, G)` of R has F, G sharing a
common *source* apex, giving `R = F° ≫ G` (F : c→a, G : c→b). Simplicity (`F°F ⊑ 1`) is not
preserved under reciprocation, so `F ≫ G°` with F,G simple is a genuinely different (and
non-book) predicate. Fixed to `R = F° ≫ G`. Downstream `S2_3.UnionOfSemiSimple` already
quantified over `F° ≫ G` directly, so it was unaffected; `S2_4.pre_positive_semi_simple_iff_metonymic`
and `S2_2` only mention `SemiSimple`/`SemiSimpleAllegory` at the type level. No proof relied on
the wrong encoding; no sorry added or removed.

## Modular law: symmetric (Dedekind) form and the Frobenius law

Freyd's allegory axiom is the **modular law** (§2.11, order form `modular_le`). §2.112 records that it is
equivalent, given the other axioms, to two one-sided forms:

    RS ∩ T ⊑ (R ∩ TS°)S          -- the stated modular identity (§2.112)
    RS ∩ T ⊑ R(S ∩ R°T)          -- its mirror (Freyd notes it is equivalent)

A fully **symmetric** equivalent intersects on both sides at once — the *Dedekind rule*:

    RS ∩ T ⊑ (R ∩ TS°)(S ∩ R°T)

It is symmetric in that applying `°` and relabeling (R ↦ S°, S ↦ R°, T ↦ T°) returns the same inequality,
whereas `(R ∩ TS°)S` singles out R. In Rel(Set) the extra intersection is free: a witness j with iRj, jSk, iTk
already satisfies i(R ∩ TS°)j (via m=k) and j(S ∩ R°T)k (via n=i), so both forms have the same content.

### The Frobenius law (Carboni–Walters) — NOT the Dedekind rule

The **Frobenius law** is a *different* statement from the symmetric Dedekind form above. Source: `Frobenius.pdf`
("Allegories and bicategories of relations"), Def 3, from Carboni–Walters [CW87, def 2.1]. An object X in a
cartesian bicategory is *Frobenius* ("discrete" in CW's terms) when its diagonal satisfies the one equation

    Δ ∘ Δ° = (Δ° ⊗ 1) ∘ (1 ⊗ Δ)

where Δ : X → X⊗X is the diagonal (comonoid comultiplication) and Δ° its adjoint X⊗X → X. This is the special
commutative Frobenius-algebra law on the diagonal. A *bicategory of relations* = cartesian bicategory in which
every object is Frobenius. `Frobenius.pdf` Prop 6: **a bicategory of relations = a unitary tabular allegory**;
its proof cites "the Frobenius law implies the modular law [CW87, remark 2.9(ii)]".

- **The book (Freyd–Ščedrov) never mentions "Frobenius".** `grep -rni frobenius` over the whole book = 0 hits.
  The term is Carboni–Walters vocabulary; Freyd's basic axiom is the modular law.
- **Advantages of Frobenius over modular:** (1) variable-free — one equation in the canonical map Δ, vs. a
  ∀-inequality over three arbitrary relations R, S, T; (2) an equality, self-dual under `°`; (3) makes the
  bicategory compact closed (Prop 4) with the diagonals as commutative Frobenius algebras, unlocking the
  string-diagram calculus and the Frobenius-algebra / TQFT body of theory.
- **Caveat (why Freyd keeps modular):** the Frobenius law lives in a cartesian bicategory, which already assumes
  comonoid diagonals Δ with right adjoints — i.e. it presupposes **unitary + tabular**. So it is not a drop-in
  replacement for modularity in a bare allegory; it is the cleaner form modularity takes in the richer
  unitary-tabular setting (Prop 6). Freyd's modular law is more primitive: it holds in *every* allegory, tabular
  or not, which is why the book takes it as basic.

Note: `Frobenius.pdf` writes composition in function order (`sr` = first r then s); this repo and Freyd use
diagram order (`RS` = first R then S). The Δ-equation above is convention-neutral.

## Allegories that are NOT unitary and/or NOT tabular

Unitary (has a unit object) and tabular (every morphism splits into a jointly-monic map pair) are two
*independent* extra conditions on a bare allegory. Standard witnesses that each can fail:

### Not tabular (but unitary): 𝒱-valued / fuzzy relations

§2.14 and §2.161: the allegory of **𝒱-valued relations between sets** for a **connected locale** 𝒱 (only
complemented elements are 0 and 1) is **not tabular**. Concretely: relations valued in the unit interval [0,1]
(fuzzy relations), or in the open-set locale Ω(X) of a connected space.

- It *is* unitary (§2.15, line 317).
- Why not tabular: maps carry only yes/no information — the book notes Map(A) ≃ Set (§2.14). A tabulated
  relation R = f°g built from maps takes only values 0 or 1, so a genuinely intermediate 𝒱-value (which
  connectedness forces to exist) can never be `f°g`. §2.168 shows you can *extend* it to a tabular allegory by
  adding graded "extent of existence" objects ⟨I, ∃⟩.

### Not unitary (and not tabular): the one-object allegories

All the §2.1 one-object examples fail unitary. A partial unit needs its identity 1_α to be the **maximum**
endomorphism (§2.15, line 309); in a one-object allegory that means identity = top, which generally fails:

- **Normal subgroups of a group** (§2.1): the monoid identity is the trivial subgroup {e}, but the top is the
  whole group G. {e} ≠ G, so 1 is not maximal → not unitary.
- **A modular lattice as an allegory** (§2.113): monoid unit ≠ lattice top in general.
- **Projective-plane allegory** (§2.157, Veblen–Wedderburn) and the **graph allegory Ĝ** (§2.158): same — a
  lattice/graph element generally has no jointly-monic map-pair splitting it, so also not tabular.

### For contrast (the axes are independent)

- §2.155's **B** (relations that are ∅ or bi-entire) is **tabular but not even an allegory** — it fails the
  modular identity. So tabularity does not imply modularity.
- §2.153's Rel(A) from partial recursive functions is **unitary and tabular but not effective** (not all
  equivalence relations split). So "unitary + tabular" is still weaker than Rel(regular category with quotients).
